# f-Socials — For Educators & Facilitators

> A short, practical guide for using f-Socials with a class, a library workshop, a newsroom, or any group learning to read media critically.

f-Socials is built for exactly this setting. It's **a lens, not a judge** — it never tells a room "this video is false," which is the wrong lesson anyway. Instead it makes the *construction* of a message visible: the claims, the evidence (or its honest absence), the persuasion techniques, and other credible angles. That's what builds a durable skill rather than a single takeaway.

## Why it works for teaching

- **No account, no setup for participants.** Anyone can open a shared report link in a browser and read it. You can run a whole session from one screen.
- **Framing-as-inoculation.** Each persuasion technique is tied to the *exact highlighted quote* that triggered it. Naming "fear appeal" on a specific sentence, a few times, is what makes learners start catching it themselves.
- **Honest uncertainty is modeled.** When evidence isn't found, the report says "no external review found" instead of bluffing. That models intellectual honesty — a hard thing to teach by lecture.
- **It invites disagreement.** Anyone can dispute a report anonymously. "Do you agree with this analysis? Where's it wrong?" is a great discussion prompt, and the dispute button makes the pushback real.

## A 30-minute lesson plan

1. **Pick a clip in advance** (a persuasive monologue or a charged news segment works well) and analyze it so the report is cached and loads instantly. Copy the share link.
2. **Open with the TL;DR only** (2 min). Ask the room: "What's this trying to get you to feel or do?"
3. **Reveal the loudest framing signal** (5 min). Show the highlighted quote. Ask: "Did you notice that the first time?"
4. **Spot-check one claim together** (8 min). Open a claim. Is it *Well-sourced* or *No external review*? Click through to the actual source and read it as a group. Discuss the difference between "no evidence found" and "false."
5. **Read one opposing perspective** (5 min). Pull up a credible source taking a different angle. Discuss how the framing shifts.
6. **Disagree on purpose** (8 min). Ask the group where the analysis itself might be wrong, and submit a real dispute together. Talk about why being able to challenge the tool matters.
7. **Send them off** (2 min) with the share link and the five-step playbook from the [User Guide](./user-guide.md#6-how-to-actually-use-this-a-short-playbook).

## Discussion prompts that travel

- "Which fact here is *true* but used to push a conclusion it doesn't support?"
- "What's missing? What context would change how you read this?" (Use the **Useful Context** cards.)
- "If you only saw the framing signals and not the claims, what would you guess the conclusion is?"
- "Where would *you* dispute this report?"

## What to be clear about with learners

- f-Socials analyzes the **transcript/text**, not visuals or tone of voice.
- **"No external review" ≠ false.** Reinforce this every time it comes up; it's the most common misread.
- Source tiers rate the **source**, never the creator of the clip.
- The report is a **starting point for judgment**, supported by human review where it exists — not a final ruling. The whole point is to hand the thinking back to the reader.
